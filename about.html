About

What this is.

Dear Stranger is a letter-based peer support site. The idea is simple: sometimes the people closest to us are the people we can say the least to.

Why I made it

I kept noticing that my friends and I would tell big, hard things to strangers on the train, in a Lyft, in a bathroom line at a party, but not to each other. Then I read about it. Psychologists actually have a name for this: the Stranger on a Train effect. The reasons we hide things from people close to us are pretty consistent. We're scared of being judged. We're scared the relationship will change ("I can't talk to my boyfriend about my boyfriend"). We're scared the story will travel. At Parsons our cohort is small enough that two questions in, everyone knows.

I looked at what already exists. Reddit gives you an audience you can't choose. Whisper is one-off lines with no real reply. 7 Cups matches you to a trained volunteer, which is good but asymmetrical: the other person is on a clock. Therapy is expensive and often months out. None of these are wrong. They just don't quite fit the specific shape of "I want one person who has been here, and I want to think about what I'm saying for a minute before I send it."

So Dear Stranger is one letter, one matched reader, one reply. You pick the reader's gender, life stage, and whether they've been through something similar. The whole thing is slow on purpose. The slowness is what makes it different from posting.

Who it's for

Right now, mostly Parsons / New School students, but anyone carrying something they can't say near home is welcome. The topics you can write about are kept narrow on purpose: relationships, identity, mental health. Legal and financial emergencies need specialists, not strangers. There's a list at the bottom of this page.

The four hardest problems

Anonymous emotional exchange has real risks. I'm not pretending these are solved, but here's how each one is being handled in the design.

1. Mental health crises

Some letters will describe self-harm, abuse, or immediate danger. A stranger's clumsy reply in that moment can do real damage. Letters that contain crisis signals (a keyword list reviewed by a licensed counselor at the Parsons Wellness Center) get re-routed to a moderator, who replies with resources and a direct line to 988. The original reader doesn't see the letter.

2. Grooming & predatory use

"Someone older who will listen" is a position predators target. Readers verify before they can respond. The thread itself is the only channel: no DMs, no email handoff, no "let's move this to Instagram." Threads close after a set number of exchanges.

3. Vicarious trauma for the people reading

Reading heavy letters week after week wears people down. Readers have a weekly cap of three letters, a cooldown after heavy replies, and a short optional care message at the end of each session.

4. Anonymity vs. accountability

Total anonymity invites trolls. Required identity defeats the point. The compromise: accounts are verified, but nothing tied to that verification is ever shown to the other person. Flags accumulate on the moderator side only.

Guidelines for replying

  • Read first, react second. Acknowledge what they said before you say anything else.
  • Don't jump straight to advice. Ask if they want it. Most of the time they don't, they just want to be heard.
  • Nothing leaves the platform. No screenshots, no quoting in your group chat. Letters stay where they are.
  • Either of you can stop, anytime. No explanation needed.
  • Edit out identifying details. Specific names, class titles, addresses: please take them out before you send.

If you're in crisis right now

Dear Stranger is not a crisis service. Please reach one of these directly:

  • 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline · call or text 988 · 24/7
  • Crisis Text Line · text HOME to 741741 · 24/7
  • The Trevor Project (LGBTQ+ youth) · 1-866-488-7386
  • NYC Well · 1-888-NYC-WELL · free, confidential, multilingual
  • Parsons Student Health Services · for any New School student

Who runs this

Right now, just me and a few volunteer moderators from the Parsons community. No ads, no tracking, no data sold to anyone. If this grows, the goal is to keep it that way.
